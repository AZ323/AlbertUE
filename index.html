Introduction

The UE (also known as the United Electrical, Radio, and Machine Workers of America) historical archive plays an essential role in understanding labor dynamics for the better part of the 20th century. Understanding its function in documenting the unions' responses to socio-political challenges helps highlight and report its activism during World War II, the impact of McCarthyism on labor movements, and the adjustments needed during the deindustrialization of the American economy. This documentation records the struggles and achievements of the UE, which serves as a lens through which we can examine the broader significance of union organizing. Understanding the historical context enables us to compare it with current labor movements, showing how the archive informs and inspires modern movements of advocacy.

Origins

The origin of the UE dates back to 1936, representing workers in the electrical manufacturing industry, a flourishing enterprise at the time. From its start, the UE fought for better wages and working conditions, unionizing against an industry known for its exploitative practices. Their mission was to address the prevalent injustice in the industry, which often prioritized profit over worker welfare. From its beginning, the UE supported the rights of its members and sought to empower workers by providing a united front against elite corporate interests that repeatedly sabotaged labor rights. As the union grew, it played a crucial role in the more expansive labor movement of the time, participating in strikes and organizing efforts that challenged societal norms. Their activism not only improved much-needed basic working conditions and laws for electrical workers but also inspired other industries to follow suit, leading to a wave of unionization across America.

WWII

During World War II, the union ensured fair treatment of workers who contributed to the war effort by producing military equipment. The archive's collection from this era showcases the union's commitment to worker rights with the nationalistic demand and patriotic motivation to increase production. The images depict scenes of bustling assembly lines and workers collaborating in teams, stressing how the spirit of the union never died even during these grueling conditions. These photos are powerful reminders of the resilience and determination of laborers who understood that their contributions were essential not only for supporting the military but also for setting post-war labor standards.

UE meeting after pearl harbor discussing labor laws for military manufacturing.

McCarthyism

One of the most challenging periods for the UE came after the war, when there was a massive rise in anti-communism, led by McCarthyism, as it impacted labor unions. The UE, with its history of supporting more socialist ideas, found itself with a target on its back. The archive from the 1950s provides valuable insight into how the union guided this political landscape, encountering accusations of communist affiliations. There was a blacklisting of union leaders, attempting to dismantle the power. Letters, transcripts of meetings, and photographs of protests against unfair hearings shed light on how the UE persisted in advocating for its members, even as it faced internal fractures and external pressure. The records being preserved help us understand how political forces shape, suppress, or transform labor movements.

This film is produced by The United Electrical, Radio and Machine Workers of America and shows how post WWII politics, which led to the Taft-Hartley Act, restricted the ability of workers to join unions.

Deindustrialization

In the latter half of the 20th century, the archive documents another significant shift, the deindustrialization of the American economy, during this time factories closed and jobs were outsourced to other countries. The manufacturing sector in the U.S. faced a dramatic downfall, the archive captures the response to this, revealing attempts to secure worker rights during manufacturer closures and layoffs. The photographs from this period display the harsh reality of abandoned factories and unemployed workers, showing the strikes of the UE to push for worker retraining programs and fight corporate policies that prioritized profits. The UE's methods during this time serve as a reminder of the need for strong labor representation in times of crisis.

Albert Smith giving a speech in the 1940s reasuring an audience of desperate workers.

Why Is It Important?

Beyond its historical significance, the archive serves as a resource for understanding and improving current labor movements. The strategies employed by the UE while facing political and economic adversity provide a blueprint for modern-day unions fighting similar battles. For instance, current labor movements in the economy, healthcare, and education all draw inspiration from the UE’s organizing methods and their persistence in advocating for worker rights despite political opposition. Modern activists see the challenges faced today such as overbearing corporate influence, threats of automation and AI, and shifting politics, by learning from the past, they can more effectively address current injustices.

Conclusion

In the current workforce, where issues like income inequality, lack of healthcare, and unstable employment remain prevalent, the role of documenting labor movements cannot be overstated. The UE archive not only preserves the history of one of America’s most important unions but also makes it clear that these struggles are never-ending. The image records and personal accounts of union members within the archive serve as powerful tools for promoting social justice by providing a tangible link between past and present struggles. By keeping these stories alive, the archive ensures that the fight for workers' rights continues, creating a better understanding of activism's crucial role in unraveling systemic inequalities.
